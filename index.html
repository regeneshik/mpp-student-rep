No Sides. Just Students.

Why I'm Running

The HSR passed a consequential resolution in the name of 855 students without asking them. The school leadership's response — a rapid public disavowal with no prior outreach to those same student representatives — was equally inadequate. One side acted without consent; the other managed the fallout without engagement. Neither was good enough.

I'm running to build something different: a representative structure that asks students first and stays honest about what it can and cannot do. I'm also running to address dysfunction that existed long before this crisis — curriculum gaps, career preparation gaps, and a school-wide silence on AI in academia that I think is genuinely embarrassing for a policy institution.

Who I Am

My Full Platform

Four pillars. Each grounded in a real gap I've experienced or heard from fellow students. None conditional on what the school leadership approves of.

01
Representation

Direct Democracy as the Default

The previous HSR's failure was not the content of the resolution — it was the process. A small coordinated group made a consequential decision for 855 students without asking them. That is a structural problem and it will happen again unless we change how the HSR operates.

Every substantive decision — any resolution, public statement, or contested position — goes to students through an open assembly before any action is taken. I coordinate and facilitate. The student body decides.

  • Regular assemblies on a fixed schedule — not just crisis responses
  • Advance notice and written submissions for each agenda item
  • Published records of decisions and reasoning after every meeting
  • Active collaboration with student groups outside the HSR to share the load
"A representative who decides for you is a manager. A representative who decides with you is an actual representative."
02
Governance

Constitutional Reform With Real Teeth

The HSR Constitution has no meaningful guardrails against political capture. Any small coordinated group can push through a resolution claiming to speak for the whole student body. That changes.

  • Mandatory consultation periods before any political or reputational resolution can be voted on
  • Supermajority thresholds for statements made in the name of the entire student body
  • Student-led drafting process — not a working group that submits proposals to administration for approval
  • Mandate boundaries grounded in the actual statutes — not the HSL's interpretation of them

On the HSL's claim that constitutional amendments require their approval: the amendment process is laid out in the constitution itself. Student self-governance subject to administrative veto is not self-governance.

"Reform that requires approval from the party you're reforming against isn't reform. It's permission-seeking."
03
Academic Quality

Curriculum, Careers & The Global South Gap

Hertie recruits globally but teaches primarily European contexts. For a large share of its student body, there is a genuine gap between what the school promises and what it delivers.

On curriculum: A student-faculty working group to open an honest, evidence-based conversation about course offerings, case studies, and reading lists — not to lower standards, but to make them genuinely global rather than Eurocentric by default.

On careers: German language support stops at B1. B1 does not get you a job in Germany. I will push for expanded language support tied to real employability — B2/C1 subsidies, career services that reflect international paths, and honest pre-arrival expectation-setting for international students.

  • Student-faculty curriculum working group with published findings
  • Career panels specifically for non-German/non-EU paths
  • B2/C1 language subsidies, not just B1 scholarships
  • Course content feedback mechanisms beyond professor ratings
04
Future of Education

AI in Academia: Stop Pretending

Hertie's assignments are designed as if AI doesn't exist. Students use it daily. The institution has no coherent response. For a school that claims to train governance leaders who will regulate and be reshaped by AI, this is a pedagogical failure — not just an integrity problem.

I founded HAIPI because this conversation needs to happen rigorously and student-led. As MPP rep, I will push for:

  • A student-faculty co-design process for an AI use framework — collaborative norms, not top-down policy
  • Assignment redesign that demands analytical reasoning, not just output generation
  • An audit of which current assignments are AI-trivial, with redesign proposals submitted to faculty
  • Open seminars bringing external speakers into the school on AI and higher education
"If your policy school trains future AI regulators but its assignments can be completed by the systems they'll regulate, something has gone seriously wrong."

The HSR Crisis: An Honest Assessment

No other candidate is saying this plainly: both sides of this crisis bear responsibility.

What The HSR Got Wrong

Passing a political resolution without student consultation was a representational failure. So was refusing to host good-faith debate despite months of visible community conflict, and aligning the formal representative body with a single political faction on one of the most divisive issues in German public life.

What The HSL Got Wrong

The first move was a public disavowal — not a private conversation. No outreach to understand the student representatives' position, no attempt to mediate before going public. Fast for reputation management. Dismissive of students. A poor precedent for how this institution handles internal disagreement.

What Needs To Change

A student council with no consultation guardrails. A school leadership that treats student governance as a reputational risk rather than a community to engage. Both are structural problems that will repeat unless directly addressed — regardless of who wins this election.

On The BDS Resolution

I will not unilaterally rescind or uphold it. Both options, taken without student input, repeat the same error that created this crisis.

First: Engage the HSL honestly — what is institutionally feasible given Hertie's position as a German university, its legal obligations, and its funding context?

Second: If an amended, non-binding human rights principles statement is credible, I'll bring it to a student assembly for decision.

Third: If rescission is the only viable institutional path, that decision goes to the student body through a transparent assembly — with constraints explained clearly. The community decides, not me.

Realism about what a student representative can actually achieve is not weakness. It is the minimum honesty I owe you.

Gaza, Germany & What I Actually Think

Vague positioning on this is a form of dishonesty. Here is where I actually stand.

Germany's Specific Context

Germany's historical responsibility for the Holocaust has created a specific legal and political environment. The Bundestag declared BDS antisemitic in 2019. Germany's domestic intelligence agency classified BDS as a suspected extremist threat. German universities with BDS-aligned statements have faced funding reviews and serious political pressure. This is the environment Hertie operates in — and no student representative can simply wish it away.

The Moral Stakes

None of the above means Gaza is not a profound moral concern — it is. Students who feel urgency about this are responding to something real. The question is not whether the concern is legitimate. It clearly is. The question is whether a student government's institutional endorsement of a specific political movement is the right vehicle for it, particularly in Germany.

The Free Speech Dimension

Every Hertie student has every right to express their views on Gaza, Israel, and BDS — clearly, loudly, as a private individual. What the HSR did was different: it used the institutional voice of the student body to endorse a political movement without that student body's consent. The question is not whether students can speak about Palestine. Of course they can. The question is whether 11 representatives can speak for 855.

What Student Representation Can Do

Israel's decisions are driven by geopolitical forces that have nothing to do with what a small German policy school's student council says. The resolution will not change outcomes in Gaza. What it did change — concretely — was the atmosphere in our community, the reputation of our institution, and the experience of students whose identities were implicated in a statement made without their consent. These are the things I can actually address.

My Position, Plainly Stated

I empathize with the motivation behind the resolution. The students who passed it were responding to something morally serious. Using the institutional voice of a German policy school's student body to endorse BDS — without consent, without space for debate, and without understanding the legal and political constraints of this country — was still the wrong call.

The school leadership's response was also wrong. Going public before attempting any direct engagement treats student governance as a liability rather than a community. That is not how a healthy institution handles internal conflict.

My commitment: Create proper spaces for this conversation — moderated, respectful, with genuine room for disagreement. Engage the HSL honestly about what is feasible. Put any formal decision about the resolution to the student body directly. This is not avoidance. It is the only process that is democratically legitimate.
